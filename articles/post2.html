Bioplastic The Synthetic Plastic Eco-Friendly Substitute

The plastic use in the entire world keep growing day by day and surely the side-effects become more and more visible in our environment. Have you ever wonder is there any substitute for synthetic plastic? Yes there is! Find out more about Bioplastic as the synthetic plastic substitute.

Read more

This is What Researchers Are Saying to Tackle Plastic Waste Problems

After knowing the effects from plastic waste in the previous article ─ Bioplastic from SDG’s POV and Its Importance, the next question is, is there any other materials that can substitute the plastic use? Yup, one of them is bioplastic. Bioplastic appeared as brand new solution to substitute the plastic use that keeps growing by time. When you try to google “Bioplastic” you will find some latest articles and researches about it.


To put it in a simple way, bioplastic is a material that has similar texture with synthetic plastic. What makes it different from the synthetic plastic is, the bioplastic naturally can get redecomposed again by microorganism as an eco-friendly compound.

Compared to the synthetic plastic, bioplastic consists of way more environmental friendly components and it reduces the carbon emission footprints by humans. Bioplastic are made from components that consist of organism polymer such as glucose, cellulose starch, and cotton either using only half of it or the entire thing (Chen, 2014). Bioplastic is biodegraddable─ability to decompose an organic compound as carbon dioxide, methane, water, inorganic component, or biomass through enzimatic mircroorganism reaction (Latief, 2001). 

 

Bioplastic is naturally easy to get decomposed in a short time and also environment friendly. 

Bioplastic can be made either by household industry or manufacturing industry. Bioplastic manufacturing industry commonly uses cassava starch that consists of high percentage of amylum (starch). Bioplastic can also be made from cellulose, PLA (Poli Lactic Acid) from cane, starch from other plants such as rice starch, corn starch, potato starch, and taro starch. Other compounds like PHB (Poly-3-Hydroxibutirat), PHA (Poly-Hydroksialkanoat), PCL (Polycaprolactone), PBS (Poly butylene succinate), and PA (Polyamide) can also be used to make bioplastic.



Bioplastic consists of biodegradable plastic (plastic that is made from fossil components) and bio-based plastic (plastic that is made from synthetic biomass or renewable energy). The connection between biodegradable and bio-based plastic can be seen from image above. Polycaprolactone (PCL) and Poly (Butylene) (PBS) based from crude oil, but they can be degraded by microorganism. On the contrary, poly (Hidroxybutyrate) (PHB), poli (Lactide) (PLA), and starch mixture are obtained from biomass or renewable energy thus they are biodegradable (Tokiwa et al., 2009).

 

How come bioplastic degradation process takes shorter time than synthetic plastic?

Usually it takes several hundred years for synthetic plastic to be able to get degraded completely whereas it only takes 3 to 6 months for bioplastic to get degraded completely. 


How come they are so different?

There are several factors that differentiate the degradation process between bioplastic and synthetic plastic, which are:

  1. Biological process. The degradation process in bioplastic use bacterias, fungis, small animals, predators, and high classified organisms.
  2. Chemical process, through hydrolysis and oxidation reaction.
  3. Mechanic process, through cleaning, using the sunlight, and climates (Chiellini, 2001).
  4. Bioplastic that consists of starch blending is easier to be degraded instead of the common synthetic plastic. There are several researches that try to degrade bioplastic using microorganisms such as fungus and bacteria. The starch blending bioplastic that is degraded using fungus utilize the carbon / C in starch first before degrading the plastic polymer. The fungus degradation rate depends on the amount of starch composition that is used as additive ingredients. As much as starch components in bioplastic, the fungus will be faster to degrade it. If the C component from the starch is already up, then the fungus will likely to use the C component from HDPE by sticking to the bioplastic surface through electrostatic charge, then using its components as the main nutrient resources.

There are some microorganisms that can convert the starch by break down the starch to glucose such as Aspergillus oryzae, Rhizopus oryzae, and Mucor sp (Ade, 2013). Fungus that can produce α-amylase and break down the bioplastic componenets are Aspergilus oryzae, Aspergillus niger, Paecilomyces subglobosum, Mucor pusilus, and Thermomyces lanuginosus (Forgaty, 1983).
